Understanding MASH

Let's talk about MASH — in words that make sense.

Medical language can make a scary diagnosis scarier. Here's a clear, human introduction to MASH and MASLD, so you can feel informed instead of overwhelmed.

Start here

What is MASH?

MASH stands for metabolic dysfunction–associated steatohepatitis. It's a more advanced form of MASLD (metabolic dysfunction–associated steatotic liver disease) — a condition where extra fat builds up in the liver.

In MASH, that stored fat is joined by inflammation and liver cell damage. Over time, ongoing inflammation can lead to scarring (fibrosis) and, if it progresses, more serious liver disease. The encouraging news: caught early, the liver has a remarkable ability to heal.

You may also hear the older names NAFLD and NASH. In 2023, an international group of liver societies updated the terminology to MASLD and MASH to better reflect the condition's link to metabolic health.

MASLD

Fat in the liver, generally without significant inflammation. The earliest, most common stage.

MASH

Fat plus inflammation and cell damage — the stage that needs closer attention and care.

Fibrosis & beyond

Scarring that can build if inflammation continues. Monitoring and lifestyle change aim to slow or reverse it.

The "silent" part

Why so many people don't know they have it

Early liver disease often has no clear symptoms. The liver rarely complains until damage is advanced — which is exactly why routine awareness, honest conversations with your doctor, and paying attention to metabolic health matter so much.

When symptoms do appear, they can be vague: fatigue, discomfort in the upper-right abdomen, or simply "not feeling right." Because these are easy to dismiss, MASH is frequently found through blood tests or imaging done for other reasons.

Know your risk

Who is more likely to be affected?

MASH is closely tied to metabolic health. Having one or more of these factors doesn't mean you have MASH — but it's a good reason to talk with your doctor.

♥

Type 2 diabetes

Insulin resistance and diabetes are among the strongest associations.

⚖

Excess weight

Particularly weight carried around the midsection.

▲

High cholesterol

Elevated triglycerides or abnormal blood lipids.

◆

Metabolic syndrome

High blood pressure and blood sugar clustered together.

Reasons for hope

The liver wants to heal

One of the most hopeful facts in all of medicine: the liver can regenerate. In the earlier stages, steady, sustainable changes can reduce liver fat, calm inflammation, and even improve scarring.

  • ✓ Nourishing, whole-food eating patterns (like the Mediterranean style)
  • ✓ Regular movement you can actually keep up
  • ✓ Managing blood sugar, cholesterol, and blood pressure with your care team
  • ✓ Limiting added sugar, refined carbs, and alcohol

Questions worth asking your doctor

Advocacy starts with the conversation in the exam room. Consider asking:

"Should my liver health be checked, given my risk factors?"
"What do my liver enzyme results actually mean?"
"Would a fibrosis assessment (like a FibroScan or blood-based test) be helpful for me?"
"What changes would make the biggest difference for my liver right now?"

MASH, in plain answers

They describe the same underlying condition. "MASH" is the updated name adopted in 2023 to emphasize the link to metabolic health; "NASH" (nonalcoholic steatohepatitis) is the term you may still see in older articles and records.

In earlier stages, many people can reduce liver fat and inflammation — and even improve early scarring — through sustained lifestyle changes and medical care. Outcomes depend on the stage and each person's health, which is why early attention matters so much. Your care team can guide what's realistic for you.

It often begins with blood tests and imaging. To assess inflammation and scarring, doctors may use specialized imaging (such as elastography/FibroScan), blood-based fibrosis scores, or in some cases a liver biopsy. Your doctor will recommend what's appropriate for your situation.
